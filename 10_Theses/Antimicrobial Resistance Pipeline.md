## Thesis Statement
Antimicrobial resistance (AMR) is projected to kill 10 million people annually by 2050 — surpassing cancer — yet the antibiotic pipeline is the thinnest it has been in 50 years. The structural problem is commercial: antibiotics are used sparingly (to prevent resistance), so revenue is capped even when the drug works perfectly. This creates a market failure that only government intervention can fix. BARDA is the key actor, consistently deploying $100M+ contracts to support novel antibiotic development. The FDA's QIDP and LPAD pathways de-risk clinical development. And the PASTEUR Act — if passed — would create a Netflix-style subscription model for novel antibiotics, unlocking the commercial ceiling. This is a patient, asymmetric thesis: most value is in monitoring the pipeline and positioning ahead of BARDA contract awards and FDA readouts.

## Core Logic
1. **The resistance crisis is structural**: WHO's 2024 priority pathogen list (Klebsiella, Acinetobacter, Pseudomonas, E. coli) includes strains resistant to all approved antibiotics — the need is existential
2. **BARDA as venture capital**: BARDA routinely deploys $50M–$500M contracts to AMR programs — these awards appear in USASpending before public announcement
3. **FDA pull pathways de-risk development**: QIDP adds 5 years of exclusivity; LPAD pathway (for unmet need) enables accelerated approval on smaller trials — changes the clinical economics
4. **Thin pipeline = pricing power**: Only 12–15 novel antibiotics are in Phase 2/3 globally — successful drugs face no competition for resistant-pathogen indications
5. **PASTEUR Act upside optionality**: If enacted, the $6B subscription model would make every novel antibiotic commercially viable overnight — binary re-rating event for all pipeline holders
6. **M&A consolidation**: Large pharma (PFE, MRK, JNJ) routinely acquire successful AMR programs — acquisition premium is the base case exit for small-cap holders
