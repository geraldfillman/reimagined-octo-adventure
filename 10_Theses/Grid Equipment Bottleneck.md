## Summary
- Grid equipment is the bottleneck layer between AI power demand and actual energized capacity.
- Utilities and power producers are demand confirmation, but the investable core here is equipment, contractors, and distribution.
- The thesis should strengthen when backlog, margin, lead-time, and contract evidence line up across multiple companies.

## Core Logic
1. Data-center and electrification demand cannot convert into load without transformers, switchgear, substations, power management, cabling, EPC labor, and distribution inventory.
2. The scarcity premium may last longer in equipment and electrical construction than in merchant power prices because supply-chain capacity is slower to add.
3. Public-market exposure is split across compounders, backlog beneficiaries, project contractors, and smaller edge names.
4. The useful signal is convergence: EIA load stress, utility capex, SEC backlog language, government funding, FMP fundamentals, and disclosure-reality confirmations.

## Invalidation Criteria
- Equipment lead times compress quickly and backlog commentary shifts from capacity constrained to normal.
- Hyperscaler capex slows enough that data-center electrical demand no longer supports the scarcity narrative.
- Contractors report backlog growth but weaker margins, labor availability, or poor project execution.
- Utility capex gets deferred by rates, regulation, or customer interconnection delays.
